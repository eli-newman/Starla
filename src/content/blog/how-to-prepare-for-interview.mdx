## Why Interview Preparation Matters

Walking into an interview unprepared is the fastest way to lose an opportunity you're qualified for. Studies show that candidates who practice answering questions out loud score significantly higher on interviewer evaluations than those who only review notes mentally.

The good news: interview preparation is a learnable skill. Whether it's your first interview or your tenth, following a structured approach makes the difference between stumbling through answers and delivering confident, compelling responses.

## Step 1: Research the Company

Before anything else, understand who you're interviewing with. This isn't just about impressing the interviewer — it helps you decide if the company is right for you.

**What to research:**
- The company's mission, values, and recent news
- The specific team or department you'd join
- The job description's key responsibilities and required skills
- Recent product launches, funding rounds, or press coverage
- Employee reviews on Glassdoor for interview format clues

Spend 30-60 minutes on this. Read the company's About page, their blog, and recent LinkedIn posts from team members. This research directly feeds into better answers.

## Step 2: Map Your Experience to the Role

Take the job description and highlight every requirement. For each one, write down a specific example from your experience — internships, class projects, part-time jobs, or extracurriculars all count.

This is where the STAR method becomes essential. For each experience, outline:

- **Situation**: What was the context?
- **Task**: What were you responsible for?
- **Action**: What did you specifically do?
- **Result**: What happened? Quantify if possible.

Having 5-7 STAR stories ready covers most behavioral questions you'll face. Learn more about the [STAR method](/blog/star-method-interviews) in our detailed guide.

## Step 3: Practice Common Questions Out Loud

Reading answers in your head is not practice. Your brain processes spoken responses differently than written ones, and interviews require you to speak clearly under pressure.

**Start with these high-frequency questions:**
1. "Tell me about yourself" (your 60-second pitch)
2. "Why are you interested in this role?"
3. "Tell me about a time you faced a challenge"
4. "What's your greatest strength?"
5. "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"

For a complete list, see our guide to [behavioral interview questions](/blog/behavioral-interview-questions).

## Step 4: Set Up Mock Interviews

Practicing alone helps, but simulating real interview pressure takes your preparation to the next level. You need to practice with:

- **Time pressure**: Real interviews don't let you pause for two minutes to think
- **Follow-up questions**: Interviewers dig deeper — you need to handle "Tell me more about that"
- **Unfamiliar questions**: You can't predict every question, so adaptability matters

Options for mock interviews:
- Ask a friend or classmate to interview you
- Use your university's career center
- Practice with an AI interview coach like [Starla](/) that gives real-time feedback

Read more about [how to practice for interviews](/blog/how-to-practice-for-interview) even without a partner.

## Step 5: Prepare Your Questions

Every interview ends with "Do you have any questions?" Saying "no" signals low interest. Prepare 3-5 thoughtful questions:

- "What does a typical day look like for this role?"
- "How do you measure success in the first 90 days?"
- "What's the team's biggest challenge right now?"
- "What do you enjoy most about working here?"

Avoid asking about salary or benefits in a first-round interview. Save those for later stages or when the recruiter brings it up.

## Step 6: Handle the Logistics

The day before your interview:

- Confirm the time, location (or video link), and interviewer's name
- Test your tech setup if it's virtual — camera, mic, internet, lighting
- Prepare your outfit the night before
- Print extra copies of your resume (for in-person)
- Get a good night's sleep

## The Day Of

- Arrive 10-15 minutes early (or log in 5 minutes early for virtual)
- Bring a notebook and pen
- Silence your phone
- Take a few deep breaths before it starts
- Remember: the interviewer wants you to succeed

## Key Takeaways

Interview preparation comes down to three things: knowing the company, knowing your stories, and practicing out loud. Most candidates skip the practice step — that's your competitive advantage.

Start your preparation today. The earlier you begin, the more natural your responses will feel when it counts.
