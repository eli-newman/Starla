## What Are Behavioral Interview Questions?

Behavioral interview questions ask you to describe specific past experiences. They start with phrases like "Tell me about a time when..." or "Describe a situation where..." Interviewers use them because your past behavior is the strongest predictor of how you'll perform in the role.

The key to answering behavioral questions is the [STAR method](/blog/star-method-interviews) — a structured framework that keeps your answer focused and compelling. Every answer below follows this format.

## The Top 10 Questions

### 1. "Tell me about yourself."

This isn't technically behavioral, but it opens almost every interview. Your answer should be a 60-second pitch connecting your background to the role.

**Framework:** Present → Past → Future. Start with who you are now, briefly cover relevant experience, and end with why you're excited about this role.

**Example:** "I'm a senior CS student at UCLA with a focus on full-stack development. Last summer I interned at a fintech startup where I built internal tools that reduced manual data entry by 40%. I'm drawn to this role because I want to work on products that real users depend on, and your team's work on the payments platform is exactly that kind of high-impact engineering."

### 2. "Tell me about a time you faced a difficult challenge."

Interviewers want to see problem-solving ability and resilience.

**Example answer:** "During my internship, our API started timing out under load during a product launch. I was the most junior engineer on the team, but I volunteered to investigate. I profiled the database queries and found an N+1 query in the order processing endpoint. I added eager loading and implemented pagination, which brought response times from 8 seconds to under 200 milliseconds. My manager started assigning me more backend optimization work after that."

### 3. "Describe a time you worked on a team."

Focus on your specific contribution and how you navigated team dynamics.

**Example answer:** "In my software engineering class, I led a four-person team building a campus event app. One teammate wasn't meeting deadlines, which was blocking the frontend. Instead of escalating to the professor, I scheduled a one-on-one to understand their situation — they were overwhelmed with another class. I redistributed their tasks and paired with them on the most critical feature. We delivered on time and got an A."

### 4. "Tell me about a time you failed."

This tests self-awareness and growth mindset. Pick a real failure, own it, and emphasize what you learned.

**Example answer:** "In my first hackathon, I tried to build too ambitious of a project and didn't scope properly. We spent 18 hours coding and had nothing working to demo. I learned that shipping something simple that works beats an impressive idea that doesn't. In my next hackathon, I scoped to an MVP first and we won second place."

### 5. "Give an example of when you showed leadership."

Leadership isn't just about titles. It's about initiative, influence, and decision-making.

**Example answer:** "As president of the data science club, I noticed attendance was dropping. Instead of guessing why, I surveyed members and found that people wanted hands-on workshops, not lectures. I restructured our meetings to be project-based — each session built part of a real ML model. Attendance went from 15 to 40+ per meeting, and three members landed data science internships using projects they'd built in our workshops."

### 6. "Tell me about a time you had to manage competing priorities."

This evaluates time management and decision-making under pressure.

**Example answer:** "During finals week last semester, I was also onboarding a new member to my research lab. I created a priority matrix and realized I could front-load the onboarding by writing a documentation guide that would save time for future onboardings too. I spent one focused evening creating the guide, then used it to onboard the new member in half the time. I finished finals with a 3.8 GPA and my advisor started using my guide for all new lab members."

### 7. "Describe a time you had to persuade someone."

Shows communication skills and the ability to influence without authority.

**Example answer:** "My capstone team wanted to use a NoSQL database, but I believed PostgreSQL was a better fit for our structured data. Instead of just arguing my point, I built a quick proof-of-concept with both databases and benchmarked them with our expected queries. The results clearly showed PostgreSQL was 3x faster for our access patterns. The team agreed to switch, and we avoided performance issues that would have hit us later."

### 8. "Tell me about a time you received critical feedback."

Demonstrates coachability and professional maturity.

**Example answer:** "During my internship code review, a senior engineer told me my code was functional but hard to read — poor naming conventions and no comments on complex logic. Initially I felt defensive, but I asked them to show me examples of well-written code in the codebase. I spent a weekend studying the team's style guide and refactoring my PRs. By the end of the internship, my code reviews went from multiple revision rounds to typically one."

### 9. "Give an example of when you had to learn something quickly."

Tests adaptability and learning ability — critical for entry-level roles.

**Example answer:** "Two weeks into my internship, I was assigned a project using GraphQL, which I'd never used. I spent the first two days going through the official docs and building a small practice API. Then I studied the company's existing GraphQL schema to understand their patterns. Within a week, I shipped my first feature — a new query endpoint for the analytics dashboard. My manager said my ramp-up was faster than expected."

### 10. "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"

Not behavioral, but appears in almost every interview. Be honest and connect it to the role.

**Example answer:** "In five years, I want to be a senior engineer who can own projects end-to-end and mentor junior developers. I'm particularly interested in scaling systems — your team's infrastructure challenges are exactly the kind of problems I want to solve. Short-term, I want to become the go-to person on my team for at least one technical domain."

## How to Prepare

1. **Write out your STAR stories** for each question category above
2. **Practice out loud** — your answer should take 60-90 seconds
3. **Record yourself** and listen back for filler words and rambling
4. **Get feedback** from a friend, career counselor, or AI practice tool

The biggest differentiator between prepared and unprepared candidates is practice. Reading these examples helps, but speaking your answers under simulated pressure is what builds real confidence.
